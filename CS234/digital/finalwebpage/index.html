Introduction

Digital Natives

Researchers have coined the terms digital-native and digital-immigrant as a way to classify and distinguish those who have grown up surrounded by technology and those who were exposed to this new medium later in their life. There are many misconceptions surrounding the correlation between age and technical advancement, with the assumption being that if a person is younger they are destined to be better at navigating technology than someone who is older. Hargittai summarizes this well in her own research, “people who have grown up with digital media are often assumed to be universally savvy with information and communication technologies” (“Digital Na(t)ives? Variation in Internet Skills and Uses among Members of the ‘‘Net Generation’’) however this is not necessarily the case as many factors such as socioeconomic status greatly affect the access to and ownership of devices like laptops and cellphones which foster the skills that a digital native may possess. For my purposes I am going to assume that I am a digital native due to my age and frequent exposure to tech devices throughout my life. My goal is to explore how to measure my personal progress as a digital native by analyzing traces from my personal data sets.

My focus

I plan to measure my growth in a number of ways by detecting patterns in the data collected from my Google Chrome Browser History as well as three Gmail Accounts I have had throughout my digital native life. I made my first Gmail account when I was about 12 years old and named it vz.gym.lax@gmail.com. Realizing while I was in high school that I needed an email account a bit more sophisticated I created viki.zygouras@gmail.com around the age of 16. Finally, once I got to college I was given my Wellesley Gmail account, vzygoura@wellesley.edu, which correlates to my age now 20. Over the past 8 years of Gmail use I plan to track my personal progress as an email writer. Through my chrome history I focus on traces between the browser and my Wellesley Gmail account. Both of these databases will help me answer underlying questions about my status as a digital native and the opportunity for change in the form of growth overtime.

Hypotheses

As a central hypothesis I am going to investigate how much growth as a digital native have I made with results from the following:

1. The lengths of my email subjects will increase overtime.

2. The lengths of my email contents will increase overtime.

3. The length of my email threads across all accounts will stay the same.

4. I check my Wellesley Email account more during week days than on weekends.

5. My style of writing emails (the language used) has stayed consistent.

Results

Hypothesis Testing

With the use of statistical analysis, I could prove my hypotheses above depending on the results of certain tests. While performing statistical analysis, I tested hypotheses 1,2, and 3 which are related to the lengths of my email subjects, content, and threads across my Gmail accounts. I predicted that the length of the content and subject of my emails will increase overtime so the null hypothesis would be that the figures are consistent overtime. The subjects of my first, middle, and last account’s sent email mean length was 14, 19, and 24 characters respectively. Thus, there was a steady growth in the average length of my email subjects but to prove they were statistically significant I ran a two-sample t-test with the mean of subject lengths for my oldest account and youngest account yielding a p-value that was less than 0.05 so I could reject the null hypothesis that my subject lengths did not change overtime. I ran similar tests to see if the difference between the content length of my oldest account and newest account were different with my oldest account having an average length of 2,772 characters and the newest account having 1,011 characters which is obvious that my email lengths did not increase overtime but rather shortened by more than 200% and the two-sample t-test affirmed this with a p-value of .014. Lastly, the average length of my email threads for each account was 1.14, 1.10, and 1.32 which does not vary much as expected.

Classification

In order to track my linguistic progress throughout the years I built two classifiers focused on the text in my subjects and the text in the contents of emails. I imported text files of every emails contents and the subject lines to use as input for the vectorizer after ensuring that the data samples from each account were the same size. The labels I used were viki12, viki16, and viki20 which correspond to one email account and also my age when that account was most active to me. Both classifiers followed the same format of inputted labels and respective text information which the count vectorizer used to determined features. I created test cases for each notebook as text that was new to the classifier to see how well it could predict which version of myself had written the email. The classifier handling my email contents incorrectly labeled 2/3 of my test cases meaning there must not have been a great distinction between the language used in each of my accounts. The same pattern followed for the classification of my email headers where the classifier’s predictions were often split evenly across each feature. For one test case from my viki12 account each feature was given the same probability of 0.333 which means that my text across all three accounts had an equal chance.

Conclusion

The methods above conclude:

1. The length of my email subjects have increased overtime.

2. The length of my email contents have decreased overtime.

3. The length of my email threads have stayed relatively the same.

4. I do in fact check my Wellesley Email account and my previous accounts more during the weekdays than the weekends.

5. My style of writing has stayed consistent overtime.

My progress as a digital native in terms of email use is directly linked to my work as a student. I am constantly checking my email accounts at peak times of the semester and then less frequently while away from school. While the length of my email contents did not increase overtime, the subjects did which indicates more descriptive titles for my emails. The drop in email content could be attributed to the fact that my younger email accounts were sometimes used as an instant messanger between my friends and I before we all had our own cellphones to use which may not indicate my progress as an email writer but I think it shows that I shift and adapt to new technologies when they become accessible. My email threads and language used in emails remaining consistent show that my email writing style has stayed fairly the same as well as use. I did not expect the email thread size to increase overtime as I primarily use email threads to communicate to professors as opposed to my classmates who I can instantly message or text. My results show that as a digitial native I am able to adapt to the new uses of different devices like cell phones as well as maintain knowledge and skills of older tools like Email.
